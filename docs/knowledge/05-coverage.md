## Trade-off Audit

Design decisions that sacrifice a lower-priority goal for a
higher-priority one. The trade-off must always be from a
lower-numbered (more important) served priority to a higher-numbered
(less important) traded-against priority. Any INVERTED row is a bug
in the tree.

| Decision | Serves | Trades against | Valid? |
|---|---|---|---|
| D-001 Strict priority ordering | P-001 | P-003 | âœ“ (1 < 3) |
| D-003 End-of-discharge guard | P-001 | P-003 | âœ“ (1 < 3) |
| D-004 Peak consumption tracking | P-001 | P-004 | âœ“ (1 < 4) |

No priority inversions.

All other D-NNN declare `Trades against: none`. These are
"pure-enforcement" or "pure-pacing" decisions that advance their
served priority without sacrificing any other. That's the expected
default; only genuine trade-offs (safety floor eating feed-in,
priority ordering forcing energy-target compromise) need explicit
declaration.

## Classification Summary

Of 45 design decisions (D-001..D-046, D-024 retired, D-041 used for
both `websocket-realtime.md` and `lovelace-cards.md`):

| Classification | Count | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| safety   | ~18 | Enforces a C-NNN invariant; non-negotiable without changing the C-NNN |
| pacing   | ~13 | Optimises a target; tunable, reviewable when assumptions change |
| other    | ~14 | Infrastructure / observability / architectural decisions |

Pacing decisions are the ones most vulnerable to priority
inversion â€” their language often borrows from safety, but they
serve targets rather than invariants. The conditional-headroom fix
to D-044 (2026-04-24) is an example of a pacing decision being
re-examined after live observation revealed it was protecting
against a scenario that the system couldn't actually reach.
